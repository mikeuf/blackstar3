Project 6 - IMC Practice

1 October, 2017

 



Pottermore is a website maintained by J.K. Rowling, the author of the Harry Potter franchise. It encourages users to create accounts and customize their own profiles. You can be "sorted" into a Hogwarts house by answering a series of questions. The website also allows visitors to discover their patronus and be picked by a magic wand.

The font is Adobe Jenson, which is similar to Magorian, the proprietary font used on the Pottermore website. It is inspired by Renaissance typography, and conveys both a sense of wonder and academia -- perfect for Hogwarts.

The screenshot is a still from the "Sorting Hat" scene of the first Harry Potter film. I used a mask to darken the top-left and bottom-right corners to create diagonals, for a sense of movement and drama, as well as to allow the font to stand out. I removed some saturation and brightened the images a bit. Desaturating is a subtle and quick way to make a photo look timeless and more emotional. The Pottermore logo was originally black, but I used a layer style called Color Overlay to change it to white.

A challenge was deciding how to position the four logos for the Hogwarts houses. I tried various configurations, including a square (two rows and two columns) as well as a single, vertical, column containing all four. These were both boring and forced me to make the logos too small. To work around this, I allowed the logos to bleed over the edges of both the artboard as well as overlapping each other, creating a looser effect. I also used various layer masks to create shadows where the logos overlap each other and to darken the area were there is some overlap with the text.

 

The image assumes that the target audience is already familiar with Harry Potter. There is no direct mention of "Harry Potter," nor is there a full explanation of what the message means, or even what the logos mean. This sort of "exclusionary" message style would make the image more effective with those who understand it, but confusing -- and possible alienating -- to those who do not. Therefore, it would make the most sense in a social media posting, where advertisers would have access to detailed audience analytics. This image would probably be most effective with people between the ages of 20 and 40 where at least some familiarity with Harry Potter can be safely assumed.

This image was created as a part of an educational project. It is not affiliated with Pottermore, or J.K. Rowling, in any way.
